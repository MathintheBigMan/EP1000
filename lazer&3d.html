The first 3D print we did was for the [chess piece] we designed. This was an assignment from the last lesson.

Here are some pictures from the print: During this stage, I just spent the first 2-3 minutes to staring at the first few layers and making sure it stuck to the bed plate. This is important as it will dictate how the rest of the print will go. After the print was finished, I had to wait as the piece was still stuck to the buid plate. Issues: After the print was done and I had to clean up the supports, this however was harder than expected as the bottom supports were harder to remove as they were closer to the edge then I anticipated. This made it so I had to leave some support material at the bottom so I do not risk breaking anything. The second print was the [Phone stand]. This print had much more issues than the first one as I will go over.

The first print went well at the start as the first few layer were flat. After 30 minutes, I came back to the print to see that the filament for the printer I was using had finished and my print was not half way finsihed when it ran out. This was my first failed print and I learnt to look behind the printer to check if the material is enought to print next time. After that, the supervisor for the T14 fablab offered to reprint my phone stand overnight and I could get it the day after. The problems did not stop however as the finished print was not without issues.

1. The top layer was slightly see through due to the problem of the printer. 2. The curved section did not turn out well on the flat side of the print. Solutions: One solution to this problem is to change the kind of supports in the slicer or remove it completely. Another is to add supports to structures I think might need it.

Laser Cutting

Preperation: For a laser begins with a sketch from (in my case) Fusion 360. Afterwards there is a promp to save drawing as a dxf file, this file is what helps the laser cutting software to recognise my 2D drawing.

 Afterwards I put those drawings into a thumb drive and load it up into the sofware used for the laser cutter which is CorelDraw. It was easy to use as I just have to drag and drop the folders and arrange them as such

 As seen from the previous picture I have also edited the outline of the drawings to be RED as it is what the laser cutter will perceive to be a vector cut with a hairline width. Also seen is the engraving logo I used for my accesory box. The logo simple has to be a different colour to the vector lines to be perceived as a raster engrave.

After setting up at CorelDraw, front he file tab there will be a print funtion, clicking on it will automatically take you to a settings tab. Make sure the printer is set to the correct Laser cutter and from print range to select current page if there is more than two files open from corel draw. One you selected Print, another software called Epilog Dashboard will come up, here I set the vector cutting and engraving settings based on the infomation sheet next to said cutter. Lastly I click the print button in the dashboard which will then transfer over the file to the laser cutter to cut.

Laser Cutter For Assignments

The laser cutter used was for the assignment the [accesesory box]. This cut went much more smooth compared to the 3D prints as there was a helpful student at the lab to always help. For this assignment only two problems came up when laser cutting:

1. During the cut the laser's thickness seemed to be too thick which would signify the printer was engraving. However after consulting with the student assistant, I found out that the height adjustment was wrong and the laser was not cutting to the intended height. This resulted in a wood finish to look like this: 2: Was the issue of some "fuzz" around the cut wood after it was laser cut. This issue was solved by adding one more round of cut with the laser to burn off the excess wood. Solutions: Asking for help was the most useful tip I have when having any sort of problems.
